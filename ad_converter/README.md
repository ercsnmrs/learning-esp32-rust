The ESP32-S3 has two distinct USB-capable interfaces. One is the native USB-OTG / USB-Serial-JTAG peripheral built into the chip (GPIO19/20). The other is a separate USB-to-UART bridge chip (CP2102, CH340, or similar) on your dev board, which connects to the chip's plain UART0 on GPIO43/44. Many S3 boards expose both as separate physical USB connectors — often labeled "UART" and "USB" (or "OTG").

Your reading works on the UART port because, by default, esp-println sends its output over UART0 — and that's exactly the line the USB-to-UART bridge is connected to. So the data flows chip UART0 → bridge chip → "UART" connector → your terminal. The native USB connector carries nothing in that configuration, because your firmware isn't routing output to the USB-Serial-JTAG peripheral.

To get output on the native USB (OTG) port instead, you have to tell esp-println to use that peripheral rather than UART. With esp-println that's a feature selection — it has a jtag-serial (USB Serial/JTAG) output mode as an alternative to its default UART mode. Switching esp-println to that feature makes the prints come out the native USB connector, at which point that port shows your ADC Val: … Voltage: lines and the UART one goes quiet.

Notes: The two ports usually enumerate as different device nodes — the UART bridge typically shows as /dev/ttyUSB0 (CP2102/CH340), while the native USB shows as /dev/ttyACM0; since you flashed over /dev/ttyACM0, your board does have the native USB active for flashing even though prints go to the UART side.
